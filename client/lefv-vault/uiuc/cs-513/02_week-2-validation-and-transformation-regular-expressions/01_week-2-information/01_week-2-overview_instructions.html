Week 2: Validation and Transformation

Overview

Data validation and transformation refer to the methods of ensuring that data conform to expected structures or formats. Data validation ensures that data match defined rules; whereas data transformations are used to convert from one structure or format to another. This week we will learn about regular expressions, a popular and widespread formalism used in both validation and transformation. A regular expression (or regex ) can be used to match patterns in text data and to extract data from the text. We will first have a brief look at where regular expressions come from (computer science theory) and then take a closer look at the practice of regular expressions. The first hands-on assignment requires the definition of some simple (or not so simple) regular expressions to match common data strings, such as dates or email addresses.

Goals and Objectives

Upon successful completion of this module, you will be able to:

  • Understand the role of validation and transformation in the data cleaning pipeline and how they can be implemented using regular expressions.

  • Interpret how regular expressions and regular languages fit in a larger theoretical framework of formal languages studied in computer science.

  • From a practical point of view, apply real-world regular expressions to match certain patterns in order to determine whether a data string corresponds to a standard format or not, say the ISO date format.

Key Phrases/Concepts

Keep your eyes open for the following key terms or phrases as you interact with the lectures and complete the activities.

  • The basic regex constructors: in theory : basic label L; and inductively defined expressions such as R|R, RR, R*.

  • In contrast, in practice we have additional operators (e.g., for convenience: R? or R+) and constructs that are crucial for getting things done in practice (e.g., character sets [..], groups (..), special characters \s, \w, \W, etc.).

  • False negatives: the regex should match, but doesn't.

  • False positives: the regex matches, but shouldn't.

  • Not everything that could (in theory) be done with regular expressions, should be done. A "division of labor" (or separation of concerns) uses regex for syntactic issues and code ("business logic") for semantics issues.

  • Regular expressions are not only useful for matching and validation of data patterns, but also for extracting and transformation of data.

Guiding Questions

Develop your answers to the following guiding questions while watching lectures and working on assignments throughout the module.

  • What are regular expressions and how can they help in the larger data cleaning and analysis life-cycle?

  • How would you describe a syntactic pattern, say for a floating point number - in theory (e.g., using a grammar) vs. in practice (using a regex)?

  • What can and can't you do with regular expressions?
