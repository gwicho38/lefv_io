CS 513: Theory and Practice of Data Cleaning Syllabus

Course Description

Welcome to CS 513: Theory & Practice of Data Cleaning!

In this course, you will learn about common data quality problems and about methods and tools to improve data quality, i.e., how to "clean" data for subsequent data analysis. Data cleaning is an essential but often under-appreciated part of data science. Some surveys report that data scientists spend the majority of their cleaning, wrangling, or “munging” data before data is actually ready to be analyzed. This course is an introduction to the theory and practice of data cleaning. We will first provide a brief overview on different dimensions of data quality. We will then turn our attention to syntactic (i.e., pattern-based) data quality issues. In the first hands-on module, we will work with regular expressions to match and extract data. We will then use OpenRefine, a powerful open source tool for cleaning individual data columns (e.g., through common transformations and operations that are used to obtain a canonical form of data). Moving on from syntactic to schema and semantic issues, we will work with both Datalog (a logic-based rule language) and SQL(ite) to express and check high-level integrity constraints. Finally, we will learn about workflow automation, provenance modeling and capture, and provenance querying, which allows users to reveal dependencies in data cleaning workflows and make data cleaning more transparent, reusable, and reproducible.

New (Summer 2024): While content (recorded lectures, assignments, etc) are ready from Week 1 (5/13/2024), we will also introduce additional materials throughout the summer, in the form of Fireside Chats , new quizzes, and problem sets. For example, we plan to provide an introduction to a powerful rule-based system ( Logica ) that can be used to validate integrity constraints (ICs) but also to query provenance and do data science analytics declaratively. These additional materials are strictly optional but provide a way (for us) to test new materials, and (for you) to obtain extra credit. More on that during the first weeks (see campuswire).

Course Goals and Objectives

Upon successful completion of this course, you will be able to:

  • Recognize common data quality problems and associate them with data quality dimensions.

  • Develop simple regular expressions for matching, extracting, and transforming data.

  • Use OpenRefine to explore and profile data, to identify data quality issues, and to clean and normalize data using its powerful built-in functions (e.g., for clustering and "fusing" similar data strings into canonical ones).

  • Use declarative constraints, expressed as Datalog (Logica) rules and SQL queries, to check the validity of datasets with respect to a given set of integrity constraints spanning one or more data tables.

  • Use a simple annotation language to create and visualize workflow models that reveal dataflow dependencies hidden in data analysis scripts in Python, R, etc.

  • Use rule-based queries on provenance graphs to explore the lineage and processing history of data from workflows and scripts.

Elements of This Course

The course is comprised of the following elements:

  • Lecture Videos. Each week, the concepts you need to know will be presented through a collection of short video lectures. You may stream these videos for playback within the browser by clicking on their titles or download the videos.

  • Weekly Quizzes : There is a quiz on the material at the end of each week. These quizzes are intended as a studying and learning activity and can be repeated with your highest score retained for the final grade.

  • Homework Assignments. There are six homework assignments in this course. Homework assignments are machine auto-graded programming assignments. You will have unlimited attempts on all homework. All homework should be submitted before the deadline. The homework is designed to allow you to gain a deeper understanding of the course content using practical exercises.

  • Data Cleaning Group Project . The data cleaning project is a conclusive project of the entire class. It requires you to integrate the knowledge and skills you gained from the entire course.

  • Online Forum and Office Hours . This course will use a Campuswire-based discussion forum. The instructor and teaching assistants will be participating in the online forum and also offer online office hours. See the Communications page for details.

Late Submission Policy

Quiz

The quizzes for Weeks 1-5 must be completed by the end of Week 6 , and the quizzes for Weeks 6-10 must be completed by the end of Week 11 . Late quizzes will receive a 20% deduction. No quizzes will be accepted after August 4th.

Homework

In this course, each homework will be released by the week started or earlier. It is highly recommended that students finish the homework by the recommended submission date, but to be flexible, we have extended the deadline one week later by default to ensure that students will have two weeks to complete each assignment. Thus, you should strictly follow the deadline of each assignment according to the table above.

  • Late assignment submissions made after the deadline will receive a 20% deduction from the total grade on the assignment.

  • Submissions made one or more weeks after the deadline will not receive any grade (0 Points) for the assignment.

With special circumstances that may delay you from completing the homework on time, you should email mcs-support@illinois.edu ahead of time to let the course team know.

Group Project

The project will be announced in Week 4. The group project is staff graded and will require time from the TA's to complete their grading. These deadlines are strict and projects handed in after their deadline will be penalized at the rate of 10% per day with a limit of 7 days.
